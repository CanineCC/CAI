The proof engine

Don't trust the number. Reproduce it.

A CAI is a claim, and a claim is only as good as its reproducibility. Take a survey, take its evidence, run the open scorer under the stated rubric version — and you should get the same number. If you don't, the claim is wrong. That is the entire point of an open standard.

How to verify a survey

  1. Get the evidence + the rubric version. A published survey states the rubric version it was scored under (e.g. rubric-2026.08.15) and links its evidence.
  2. Run the reference scorer from CanineCC/CAI over that evidence, pinned to the same rubric version.
  3. Compare. Same evidence + same rubric ⇒ the same 0–100 and band. A mismatch is falsifiable proof the published number is wrong.
# reproduce the headline the survey published, from its evidence bundle
dotnet run --project Cai.Cli -- verify survey-evidence.json
# → ✓ reproduced: CAI 72 (Strong) under rubric rubric-2026.06.0 (claimed 72, Δ0.00)

# a number that doesn't follow from the evidence fails (exit 1)
dotnet run --project Cai.Cli -- verify survey-evidence.json --expect 90
# → ✗ MISMATCH: evidence folds to 72.20 but the bundle claims 90.00 (Δ17.80 > 0.50)

The weights are published inside the evidence, so the fold is exact: same bundle ⇒ the same number, for anyone, with no access to the analyzer.

Signed, so attribution is checkable too

A published survey is cryptographically signed by its issuer — so beyond reproducing the number, you can confirm who attested it. The signature covers the content hash; the public key is published; anyone can verify the signature independently. Openness proves the method; the signature proves the author.

Every record in the registry can be verified this way — number and author both. No logo wall, just falsifiable records.
